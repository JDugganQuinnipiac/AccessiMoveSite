Assistive Software

AccessiMove is an innovative assistive software designed to empower individuals with disabilities or limited hand mobility by providing hands-free control of computer systems. Through facial tracking and gesture recognition technologies, AccessiMove enables precise mouse control and intuitive interaction with computers. This poster presents an overview of AccessiMove's features, methodology, results, and conclusions, highlighting its potential to enhance accessibility in computing environments

Applications

  • Hands-free control of computer systems
  • Precise mouse control through facial tracking and gestures
  • Alternative input methods without traditional devices
  • Intuitive interaction with computers
  • Eye, gaze, and head tracking for diverse inputs
  • Customizable settings for individual preferences
  • Gesture mapping for assigning actions to gestures
  • Accessibility across computing tasks and gaming

Features

AcessiMove employs MediaPipe to generate facial landmarks for each video frame, enabling three main tracking methods: Gaze, Head, and Eye. Changes in these landmarks between frames enable the software to execute the following methods

Gaze-Tracking

Calibration involves facing corners and center of the screen to record the position of the nose bridge landmark (P1) and derive P2, P3, P4, P5, and P6. Coordinates of these points are mapped to screen dimensions using linear interpolation. Moving P1 within the calibrated points directs the on-screen cursor to follow suit.

Head Tracking

This system tracks head movements—nodding up/down or tilting left/right—using nose tip (P1) and chin (P2) landmarks. It computes vector V = P1P2 and angles θ1 (pitch) and θ2 (roll) between V and the positive z-axis and y-axis, respectively. Changes beyond a threshold trigger corresponding actions.

Eye-Detection

Three landmarks around the eye—upper eyelid center (P1), lower eyelid center (P2), and a point lateral to P2 (P3)—are used. By calculating D1 = |P1 – P2| and D2 = |P2 – P3|, software computes R = D1 / D2. As the eye closes, D1 decreases, causing R to drop below a threshold, signaling eye closure. A frame counter tracks closed-eye instances. If closed for a set number of frames, the corresponding action is triggered, unless both eyes blink, which is detected separately.
